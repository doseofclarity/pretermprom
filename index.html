Preterm PROM

Preterm Premature Rupture of Membranes – rupture of the amniotic sac before 37 weeks gestation and before the onset of labor. Major cause of perinatal morbidity. Master diagnosis, management, complications, and nursing care.

Definition & Significance

Preterm PROM (PPROM) is the spontaneous rupture of the fetal membranes before 37 weeks of gestation and before the onset of labor. It occurs in approximately 3% of pregnancies and is responsible for about one‑third of all preterm births.
  • Consequences: Preterm labor, chorioamnionitis, placental abruption, cord prolapse, neonatal sepsis, RDS, and long‑term neurodevelopmental impairment.
  • Gestational age matters: Outcomes improve with advancing gestational age; management differs significantly before 34 weeks vs after 34 weeks.

Risk Factors & Etiology

👩 Maternal risk factors
• Prior preterm birth or PPROM
• Genital tract infection (bacterial vaginosis, cervicitis, STIs)
• Short cervical length (<25 mm)
• Cervical insufficiency
• Uterine overdistension (multiple gestation, polyhydramnios)
• Smoking, substance abuse, low socioeconomic status
🦠 Pathophysiology
• Infection/inflammation (ascending from vagina) → release of proteases and inflammatory cytokines → membrane weakening and rupture.
• Decidual hemorrhage or uterine overdistension may also cause mechanical stress leading to rupture.

Diagnosis & Confirmation

  • Clinical presentation: Sudden gush or persistent leakage of fluid from the vagina.
  • Diagnostic tests:
    • Sterile speculum exam – visual pooling of fluid in the posterior fornix.
    • Nitrazine (pH) test – amniotic fluid turns nitrazine paper blue (pH 7.1–7.3).
    • Ferning test – amniotic fluid crystallizes into fern‑like pattern on microscope slide.
    • Ultrasound – low amniotic fluid index (oligohydramnios) supports diagnosis.
  • Differential: Urine, vaginal discharge, cervical mucus, watery leukorrhea.
  • Avoid digital cervical exam until active labor or delivery is planned – reduces infection risk.
⚠️ Important: Once PPROM is confirmed, assess for signs of infection (chorioamnionitis) and fetal well‑being.

Management & Interventions

  • General principles: Hospitalization; monitor for infection, labor, and fetal well‑being.
  • Latency antibiotics: Ampicillin + erythromycin (or azithromycin) for 7 days to prolong latency and reduce chorioamnionitis and neonatal morbidity.
  • Corticosteroids: Betamethasone or dexamethasone (24–34 weeks) to accelerate fetal lung maturity.
  • Tocolysis: May be considered for 48 hours to allow corticosteroids to take effect, if no infection and fetal benefits outweigh risks.
  • Gestational age‑based management:
    • Before 34 weeks: Expectant management with antibiotics and corticosteroids.
    • 34–36⁶⁄⁷ weeks: Expectant management or induction depending on maternal/fetal status.
    • ≥37 weeks: Term PROM – induction recommended.
  • Chorioamnionitis: Prompt delivery regardless of gestational age; broad‑spectrum antibiotics.
  • Group B Streptococcus (GBS) prophylaxis – indicated after rupture.

Maternal & Fetal Complications

👩 Maternal
• Chorioamnionitis (fever, uterine tenderness, maternal/fetal tachycardia)
• Endometritis, sepsis
• Placental abruption
• Postpartum hemorrhage
👶 Fetal/Neonatal
• Preterm birth complications (RDS, IVH, NEC, sepsis)
• Pulmonary hypoplasia (if prolonged oligohydramnios)
• Cord prolapse (especially if malpresentation)
• Neonatal infection (early‑onset sepsis)
  • Patient education for PPROM: Hospitalization likely; strict bed rest not proven beneficial but avoid digital stimulation; report fever, chills, abdominal pain, foul discharge; understand signs of labor and infection.

Nursing Care & Prevention

  • Assessment: Monitor vital signs (fever), uterine tenderness, fetal heart rate, leakage amount, contraction frequency, signs of chorioamnionitis.
  • Interventions: Administer antibiotics, corticosteroids, tocolytics as ordered; maintain strict perineal hygiene (no tampons, no intercourse, no digital exams); provide emotional support and education.
  • Prevention strategies: Treat bacterial vaginosis and other infections; progesterone for short cervix; cervical cerclage for cervical insufficiency; smoking cessation.
  • Discharge teaching if sent home (rarely, only with stable, very early PPROM without infection): Daily temperature monitoring, fetal kick counts, report any fever, fluid leakage increase, contractions.
📌 Documentation: Time of rupture, fluid characteristics (color, odor), fetal heart rate pattern, maternal vitals, medications given.
